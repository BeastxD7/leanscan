# Engagement — how to reply, quote-tweet, and DM in a way that compounds

Your tweets are 50% of your X presence. Your replies are the other 50%, and they're the half most indie founders neglect.

These templates aren't scripts. They're patterns. Adapt to context.

---

## When someone tweets about MyFitnessPal frustration

> "This is exactly why I'm building LeanScan. Logging a meal in MFP takes longer than eating it. Built mine around an AI photo flow — under 10 seconds end-to-end. Beta soon if you want to try it."

Don't always pitch. 4 out of 5 times, just commiserate ("yeah, the search UX is brutal"). 1 in 5, mention LeanScan.

---

## When someone tweets about losing weight on a GLP-1

If they're celebrating:

> "Congrats on the progress. One thing that's worth flagging if you haven't already: GLP-1 cuts often eat into muscle if protein isn't deliberately tracked. 1.6g/kg is the rough number. It's the gap I'm trying to close with [link]."

Be careful not to be the guy who shows up to celebrate threads to sell something. Earn the right to mention LeanScan by leading with their wins and only flagging the muscle-loss point if it's genuinely useful.

If they're struggling:

> "Sorry that's been your experience. If you DM me I'll get you a TestFlight invite — built LeanScan specifically for the protein-tracking gap on GLP-1. Free, no card needed for beta."

---

## When another indie founder ships something

If it's good:

> "[Specific praise about a specific thing.] Bookmarked."

> "The way you handled [specific UX problem] is exactly what I needed to see this week. Stealing the pattern (with credit)."

If it's not quite there:

> "Solid v1. Curious how you're thinking about [specific challenge they'll hit] — that's the part I've been stuck on for LeanScan."

Don't critique unsolicited. Don't quote-tweet to dunk. Founders remember.

---

## When a journalist or larger account engages

Reply within an hour if you can. Be normal. Don't be eager.

> "[Honest answer to their question]. Happy to chat more if useful — [your DMs open / email in bio]."

Don't say "DM me for a press kit." Say what you'd say to a friend.

---

## When someone replies to your tweet

**Always reply.** For the first 1000 followers, every single reply.

Reply patterns:

- If they ask a question → answer it specifically. Don't dodge.
- If they share a similar experience → ask a follow-up question. Compounds the conversation, signal to algorithm.
- If they push back on your take → engage honestly. "Fair point. Where I disagree is…" beats defensiveness.
- If they say something nice → "Thanks 🙏" is fine. Don't oversell.
- If they spam → don't reply, don't engage. Block if it's persistent.

---

## When you want to DM a stranger

The format that works:

> "Hey [Name] — saw your thread about [specific thing]. I'm building [LeanScan / your one-liner] which touches on [the thing they cared about]. No pitch — just genuinely curious if you'd be willing to try a beta and give 10 minutes of feedback. TestFlight link here: [link]. No worries if not."

What this DM does well:
- Opens with a specific thing they did (not "hey founder")
- States who you are and what you're building
- Asks for something small and specific (10 minutes)
- Includes the link so they don't have to ask
- Gives them an explicit out

What to avoid:
- "I'd love to pick your brain"
- "Quick question"
- "Hey, I think we should collaborate"
- A wall of text before getting to the ask

---

## Quote-tweet vs reply — when to use each

**Quote-tweet** when:
- You have a genuine 1-2 sentence add to the original tweet
- You're sharing the original because your audience would benefit
- You want to bring the original into your timeline permanently

**Reply** when:
- You're directly engaging with the original author
- Your comment doesn't stand alone without the original context
- You want the author to see it more than your own audience

Default to reply. Quote-tweets that say "this 👆" or "💯" pollute your timeline.

---

## When someone signs up for the beta

Reply / DM them ASAP. Within 5 minutes if you can. This is when they're most engaged.

> "Welcome 🙏 You're beta user #[N]. A few things:
>
> 1. TestFlight invite is in your inbox.
> 2. I read every piece of feedback you send.
> 3. If something's confusing or broken, please tell me — that's the most useful thing right now.
>
> Looking forward to seeing what you think."

---

## When a beta user gives you feedback

Bad reply: "Thanks for the feedback!"

Good reply: "[Specific acknowledgment of their specific point]. The reason it works that way is [honest answer]. I'm going to look at [the specific change you're considering]. Will report back."

Then *actually* report back when you ship the change. "Hey [Name] — shipped the change you mentioned. It's live in build 1.2. Thanks again for the catch."

This is how 10 beta users turn into 10 evangelists.

---

## The "founder is responsive" effect

The single highest-leverage thing you can do in the first 1000 followers / 100 users is reply fast and reply often. Not because the algorithm rewards it (it does, slightly). Because **humans remember founders who replied to them.**

Some of the most loyal users of indie products are users who once sent feedback and got a personal reply within an hour. They tell other people. They show up in your launches. They become advocates without being asked.

Cost: maybe 20 minutes a day for the first few months. Returns: customer LTV that no ad campaign can match.
